Introduction¶

For this project, we use coffee_survey.csv, a dataset released by TidyTuesday on 14 May 2024. The dataset comes from The Great American Coffee Taste Test, an online tasting event organised by James Hoffmann and Cometeer in October 2023 (Hoffmann, 2023). In this event, around 4,000 people in the United States brewed four blind-labelled coffees at home while following a YouTube livestream, and then filled in a survey about their demographics, brewing habits, favourite drinks, self-rated coffee expertise, and monthly coffee spending. The final dataset contains 4,042 responses and 57 variables. An early summary of the survey noted that respondents were more likely to be younger, male, and interested in lighter roasts, compared with the broader population of coffee drinkers (McKeon Aloe, 2023). The dataset was also later used by Maven Analytics (2024) as an open visualisation challenge.

In this report, we are mainly interested in coffee spending. More specifically, we want to see which demographic factors, coffee preferences, and self-rated expertise are linked to higher spending in this sample. We break this into three smaller questions:

  1. Demographics. How do age and education level relate to monthly coffee spending?
  2. Brewing method and favourite drink. Which combinations are associated with higher spending?
  3. Self-rated expertise. Does higher expertise predict more spending on equipment, on coffee, or on both?

To answer these questions, we first clean the dataset and select the variables that are most relevant to spending, including age, education level, brewing method, favourite drink, expertise, and equipment spending. We then use three figures to examine the data from different angles: a grouped bar chart for demographic patterns, a heatmap for drink and brewing preferences, and a line chart for expertise and equipment spending. However, this dataset is based on a convenience sample, since participants chose to join a coffee-themed YouTube event. This means the sample likely contains more coffee enthusiasts than the general public. Because of this, the results in this report should be read as patterns within this sample rather than causal conclusions or estimates for all coffee drinkers in the United States.

Data Cleaning¶

The coffee dataset file was loaded from Github, a copy of the data was made, then duplicate values were dropped if applicable. To answer the three questions, these variables were carefully chosen: age, total_spend, brew, favorite, expertise, and spent_equipment. These were chosen after further exploration of the data: the sampling of this dataset is biased towards young adults, full-time employees, Caucasians, and people who have Bachelor degrees. Since the dataset had many missing values, a summary of missing values was made to check if there is enough data for each variable to carry on with further analysis.

Figure 3- Line Plot¶

For the last figure, a line plot was used. This is to illustrate the relationship between self-rated coffee expertise vs. amount spent on coffee equipment, with a slight caveat- there is an additional hue variable of total amount of coffee spent per month to subset this relationship. The total_spend variable was collapsed into three categories just like in figure 1, the spent_equipment variable, which is a categorical variable showing a range of prices, is converted into a numerical variable by taking its midpoint, so that the plot is able to take the average amount of respondents that spend on coffee equipment. In addition, a secondary y-axis is included on the right side of the plot to display the count of observations at each expertise level.

Discussion¶

Figure 1- This figure shows that people spend more on coffee per month during the working ages between 25 and 54. This makes sense in a post-COVID, western societal context- it can be assumed that people pay for more coffee for a caffeine boost during a working day. Another assumption is that people may pay for more coffee at outside establishments (requiring extra service fees), rather than their homes in a working day due to its convenience.

Figure 2- The results show that pourover and lattes are the most commonly reported favorite drinks. People typically spend $20-$60 a month on coffee, and this is prevalent in most drinks, especially pourovers, regular drip coffee and lattes. People typically would not too much money over their favorite coffee drink. This suggests that people would pay for good quality coffee, without breaking the bank.

Figure 3- This plot a generally positive association between self-rated coffee expertise and the amount spent on coffee equipment. Each line represents a subgroup defined by monthly coffee spending, while the y-values correspond to the average equipment expenditure (approximated using the midpoint of each price range) at each expertise level.Overall, respondents with higher self-rated expertise tend to invest more in coffee equipment. However, this does not necessarily imply that they also spend more on coffee per month. For instance, the group spending less than $40 per month (blue line) exhibits relatively high equipment spending at higher expertise levels (e.g., around rating 9). This suggests that more experienced coffee drinkers may shift their consumption patterns from purchasing coffee externally to investing in home brewing equipment, which can be more cost-efficient in the long run.Additionally, a secondary y-axis is included to show the number of observations at each expertise level, providing context for the stability of the estimated averages.

Conclusion¶

In conclusion, it is shown that age, favourite coffee drink, and the relationship between self-rated coffee expertise vs. amount of equipment spent has an affect on how much a respondent spends on coffee per month. In particular for those who spend more on coffee, it is typically those who are young adults, people who enjoy pourovers and lattes, those who rated themselves highly in terms of coffee expertise, and those who spent more on coffee equipment. It can be assumed that people pay for more coffee due to the following factors: convenience of outside establishments and a caffeine boost during the working day. Also, respondents would pay for good quality coffee but are sensible with their spending. Lastly, a strong investment in coffee equipment does not mean people spend more on coffee per month- perhaps it's more worth it to make coffee at home.
